# Final Recommendation

## Executive Summary

A multiple regression analysis was conducted to identify the factors most strongly associated with monthly sales across retail stores. The final model explained approximately **80.74% of the variation in monthly sales (R² = 0.8074)**, indicating that it provides a strong explanation of sales performance. The analysis shows that customer footfall, marketing spend, inventory availability, and regional differences are important factors influencing monthly sales.

---

## 1. Which Factors Appear Most Strongly Associated with Monthly Sales?

Among all variables included in the analysis, **footfall** showed the strongest positive association with monthly sales. It had the largest statistically significant coefficient and the strongest explanatory power in the simple regression model (R² = 73.63%).

**Marketing spend** also demonstrated a positive and statistically significant relationship with monthly sales, suggesting that investment in marketing contributes to increased revenue.

**Inventory availability** had a positive and statistically significant effect, indicating that maintaining adequate stock levels supports higher sales by reducing stockouts.

The **East region** dummy variable showed a statistically significant negative relationship with monthly sales when compared with the West region, suggesting regional differences in store performance.

---

## 2. Which Variables Should Leadership Focus On?

Based on the regression results, leadership should prioritize the following areas:

* **Increase customer footfall** through targeted marketing campaigns, customer engagement initiatives, loyalty programs, and in-store promotions.
* **Maintain effective marketing investment**, as higher marketing spend is associated with increased sales.
* **Improve inventory availability** to ensure products remain in stock and customer demand can be met consistently.
* **Review the performance of stores in the East region**, where sales are significantly lower than those in the West region after controlling for other business factors. Additional investigation may help identify operational or market-specific challenges.

These variables demonstrated statistically significant relationships with monthly sales and offer practical opportunities for improving business performance.

---

## 3. Which Variables Should Not Be Over-Interpreted?

The analysis indicates that **Region_North** and **Region_South** should be interpreted cautiously because their p-values exceeded the 5% significance level. This means there is insufficient statistical evidence to conclude that stores in these regions perform differently from stores in the West region after accounting for the other variables.

Similarly, the simple regression models showed that **average discount percentage** and **customer rating** were not statistically significant predictors of monthly sales when analyzed independently. These variables should not be considered primary drivers of sales based on the current analysis.

---

## 4. Recommended Business Actions

Based on the findings, the following actions are recommended:

* Increase initiatives that attract more customers to stores, as customer footfall is the strongest driver of sales.
* Continue investing in marketing while regularly evaluating campaign effectiveness and return on investment.
* Maintain high inventory availability to reduce lost sales caused by stock shortages.
* Conduct a detailed operational review of stores in the East region to identify opportunities for performance improvement.
* Continue monitoring sales performance and incorporate additional business variables into future analyses to improve prediction accuracy.

---

## 5. Risks and Limitations

Although the multiple regression model explains a large proportion of sales variation, it does not capture every factor influencing monthly sales.

Potential limitations include:

* Promotional campaigns and pricing strategies were not included.
* Seasonal demand and holiday effects may influence sales.
* Customer demographics and purchasing behaviour were not considered.
* Local competition and economic conditions may vary across regions.
* Store management quality and customer service were not measured.

As a result, the model should be used as a decision-support tool rather than as the sole basis for business decisions.

---

## 6. Why Regression Shows Association but Not Causation

Regression analysis identifies **statistical associations** between variables but does **not automatically prove that one variable causes another**.

For example, the analysis found that stores with higher footfall tend to generate higher sales. However, regression alone cannot determine whether increased footfall directly causes higher sales, or whether another factor—such as stronger marketing campaigns, better store locations, or seasonal demand—contributes to both higher footfall and higher sales.

Similarly, while marketing spend is positively associated with sales, the analysis cannot conclude that increasing marketing expenditure will always produce proportional increases in sales. Controlled experiments or additional business analysis would be required to establish a causal relationship.

Therefore, the regression results should be interpreted as evidence of meaningful relationships that can guide business decisions, while recognising that other unmeasured factors may also influence monthly sales.

---

## Final Recommendation

The multiple regression model is recommended as the preferred model because it explains approximately **80.74%** of the variation in monthly sales and provides a comprehensive understanding of the key business drivers. Leadership should focus on increasing customer footfall, maintaining effective marketing investment, and ensuring high inventory availability, while conducting further analysis of regional performance—particularly in the East region. Future models should incorporate additional operational and market variables to further improve predictive accuracy and support more informed business decision-making.
